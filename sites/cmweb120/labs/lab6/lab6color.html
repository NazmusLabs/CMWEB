This lab involves working with images and colors. The visual presentation of a website is one of the most important considerations, among with the actual content itself, when building a website. The reasoning for this all goes back to ensuring the user experience (UX) of the website is first class.

How a website looks isn't just about flashy colors and animations or to show off one's artistic abilities. The goal of creating the visual presentation should always to provide the user/visitor with a delightful experience that puts its central focus on the content. Therefore, decisions such as where to apply an accent color or how to animate in a prompt to the user's attention should all preferably be made to achieve that goal.

Photo Gallery

Finding the Right Balance with Color

Color can be a powerful tool in creating a good user experience. Accent colors highlight menus, buttons, and other UI (user interface) elements so that the user can quickly find the appropriate link or button to navigate or interact with the website. At the same time, it should not be so vibrant or overemphasize parts of UI that it veers the visitor's attention away from the main content of the webpage. Because the human eye naturally focuses on the brightest object in a scene, the main content of the page should always have the spotlight while still maintaining enough emphasis on the UI so that the user can quickly spot them when they wish to navigate to another section of the website, for example.

Example of a Design Consideration

With the previous lab, Lab 5, I have redesigned the nav-bar that I have been using in the CMWEB 120 website thus far. In webpages prior to lab 5, although I have made minor adjustments, the overall look and feel of the nav-bar remained relatively the same. Before Lab 5, the main nav-bar sported a dark shade of grey, close to the dark background of the webpage, visible on the header and footer. The similar shading allowed the nav-bar to nicely blend in with the header while remaining distinct. The subtleties allowed the brighter content section to shine. With lab 5, I attempted to apply a new color scheme to the nav-bar, and in doing so, I had to ensure that I do not take away from what worked in the previous design.

It is for this reason that the majority of the time spent in redesigning the nav-bar was trying to find the appropriate color-gradient combinations. Because the accent color I chose was orange, it was relatively easy to pick a color that was overly fluorescent, enough to distract the reader from the featured article on the page. Furthermore, the gradient and drop shadow needed to be subtle enough to not look too skeuomorphic and calling unnecessary visual attention to it. (I could have gone for a completely flat design, forego shadows and gradients altogether, but I believe a slight amount of them on relevant UI elements is necessary.) Additionally, the colors of the disabled and hover states of the menu items also needed extra attention. They needed to work well with the shade of the color and the amount of gradient the nav-bar was using. All in all, finding the right balance took more time than all other aspects of the redesign, such as new fonts, spacing, etc, combined!
